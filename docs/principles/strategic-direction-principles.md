# Strategic Direction Principles

Version: v1.0

These principles define the organization's standards for strategic decision-making within the AIEOS framework. They are input material for artifact generation — not governed artifacts themselves.

---

## 1. Bets Over Plans

Strategic direction is expressed as falsifiable bets, not comprehensive plans. A bet has a thesis, a time horizon, and explicit success and failure conditions. A plan pretends to predict the future. Bets acknowledge uncertainty; plans hide it.

## 2. Failure Signals Are Mandatory

Every strategic bet must define what would cause the organization to stop investing. If leadership cannot articulate a failure signal, the bet is not strategic — it is a commitment that has bypassed scrutiny. The failure signal is the primary anti-politics mechanism.

## 3. Strict Prioritization Over Consensus

Portfolio prioritization produces a strict rank order, not tiers or equal-priority groupings. Tiers are a mechanism for avoiding hard choices. If two bets cannot be distinguished in priority, the portfolio owner must decide which one would be funded if only one could be. Consensus-driven prioritization produces bloated portfolios where everything is "P1."

## 4. Capacity Honesty

Funded bets must fit within stated capacity. Over-commitment is not ambition — it is a planning failure that cascades into execution quality problems downstream. The cut line is the mechanism for capacity honesty: bets below the line are deferred, not squeezed in.

## 5. Signal-Driven Review

Re-prioritization happens when signals warrant it, not solely on a calendar cycle. Calendar-based review is a backstop, not the primary trigger. IEK feedback, SBR time horizon expiry, market events, and capacity changes are all valid triggers for re-evaluation.

## 6. Lean Over Comprehensive

Strategic direction governance uses two artifacts (SBR and PPR), not five. The value of strategic governance comes from forcing falsifiability, measurability, and prioritization honesty — not from comprehensive strategy documentation. More artifacts mean more ceremony, more politics, and less honesty.
